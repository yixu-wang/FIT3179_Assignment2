A glass half-full?
Shifting attitudes towards alcohol consumption in Australia
Alcohol holds an undeniable place in Australian culture. Consumed by roughly three-quarters of Australians 14 years and above [1], alcoholic beverages are arguably some of Australians' favourite drinks. Yet our love of alcohol also makes us particularly susceptible to the harms that stem from the abuse of this euphoric substance. To address this, numerous strategies, such as the government's National Drug Strategy [3], have been developed in part to reduce the risky consumption of alcohol. But just how successful have these policies been?

A quick glance at the data through time shows that rural populations are generally riskier drinkers, with the outback regions in Western Australia, South Australia, and the Northern Territory consistently remaining hotspots throughout the recorded period. Several potential reasons have been proposed, including the strong drinking culture in remote communities, the lack of entertainment options, and limited access to health services [3].

Conversely, the map also suggests that heavy consumption is falling in the cities, particularly in Victoria, New South Wales, and the Australian Capital Territory. Whether this is due to effective messaging is impossible to determine from the visualised data. However, the data does suggest a need for a different approach towards rural populations - one that addresses the issues mentioned above.

We can also divide the population based on their drinking behaviours and analyse the characteristics of each subpopulation. Of particular interest are the younger age groups (14-17 and 18-24), who are more vulnerable to alcohol misuse due to social pressure and curiosity, while also most susceptible to the harms of alcohol due to their developing brains [4].

While adolescents (14-17) in general are extremely unlikely to participate in risky drinking, young adults of both genders (18-24) are the heaviest drinkers of all age groups, albeit by a small margin for men. These statistics reinforce the idea that messaging should be primarily targeted towards young people, especially when considering their status as a vulnerable population.

Perhaps unsurprisingly, men are more likely across the board to drink heavily than women; suggested reasons for this include genetics, marketing, and peer pressure [5].
Consumption over Time
Perhaps the most interesting of all is how risky alcohol consumption has changed over the past two decades. Almost all age groups are now consuming less alcohol when compared to 2002 statistics, with 60-69 year olds being the only exception.

We can also see that underage drinking is actually on the decline, and is part of a decades-long trend which has seen the proportion of risky teenage drinkers go from 30.7% to just 5.5%.

A similar trend can be observed in young adults, although not to the same extent. The proportion of such drinkers has also appeared to have stabilised at around 41% since 2016.

In fact, these patterns reflect a "global shift", according to the Unviersity of New South Wales [6], which affirms that the phenomenon is not due to increased consumption of illicit drugs. Does that imply, then, that in the near future, young people will be completely healthy free of any drugs?

Alcohol and e-cigarette use in youth

Dataset: AIHW National Drug Household Survey 2022-2023: Table 3.15 Electronic cigarettes and vapes: https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/illicit-use-of-drugs/national-drug-strategy-household-survey/data?&page=1
Unfortunately, things are unlikely to be that simple. For one, young adult alcohol use is stagnant, rather than decreasing, and remains the highest among all age groups. In addition, however, a new product has also begun to gain popularity in recent years: e-cigarettes, or "vapes".

These cigarette replacements often contain nicotine and artificial flavourings, and are perceived by those who use them to be less harmful than cigarettes - on par with "drinking alcohol, eating junk food, and using cannabis" [7]. However, while research on the harms of vaping is limited, growing evidence several potential harms, such as lung damage, increased risk of cancer, and nicotine addiction [8].

Despite this, e-cigarettes have proven to be especially popular with young adults, with the proportion of users growing from just 2.8% to 20.6% in just 6 years. This may indicate the emergence of an alarming new trend, which requires new messaging to combat effectively.

It may be reassuring that strategies against vaping have already begun to take shape, with a new national campaign targeting young people launched in June of this year [9]. Of course, their effectiveness is yet to be proven.
Summary
Overall, while the effectivessness of the government's messaging on alcohol use cannot be evaluated directly, it also cannot be denied that alcohol use is essentially at an all time low for many age groups, especially teenagers.

Of course, more can always be done, and rural populations and young adults have shown relatively resistant to this trend in recent years, likely due to the likely due to the unique and distinct pressures faced by these two groups. This indicates the need for more refined messaging and strategies which address the root cause of the groups' alcohol usage.

Additionally, it appears that young adults are increasingly turning away from alcohol towards vaping; this necessitates its own messaging, and only time will tell whether similar results can be achieved.
